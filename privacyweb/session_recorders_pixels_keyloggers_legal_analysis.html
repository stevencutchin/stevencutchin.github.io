Session Recorders

FullStory, Hotjar, LogRocket, Mouseflow, Crazy Egg

What Are Session Recorders?

Session recording tools capture and replay user interactions on websites, creating a video-like recording of browsing sessions. They monitor: mouse movements (every pixel traveled), clicks (what you clicked and where), scrolling (how far, how fast), form inputs (what you type, including deletions), page navigation (every page visited), time spent on each element.

// Typical session recorder implementation <script> // FullStory example - runs immediately on page load window['_fs_debug'] = false; window['_fs_host'] = 'fullstory.com'; window['_fs_script'] = 'edge.fullstory.com/s/fs.js'; window['_fs_org'] = 'YOUR_ORG_ID'; (function(m,n,e,t,l,o,g,y){ // Captures EVERYTHING without asking // Mouse, keyboard, scrolling, clicks, forms })(window,document,window['_fs_namespace'],'script','user'); </script>

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Analysis: Do Session Recorders Require Consent?

YES - Strong Legal Requirement for Consent

Session recorders almost certainly require explicit consent under multiple legal frameworks. This is one of the clearest cases for requiring consent.

üá∫üá∏ California Penal Code ¬ß 631 (WIRETAP)

Applies: YES CRITICAL VIOLATION

  • The Law: Prohibits intentionally tapping or making an unauthorized connection to any electronic communication, or willfully and without consent reading or attempting to read any message while in transit
  • Why It Applies: Session recorders intercept electronic communications (user interactions with website) in real-time as they occur
  • Key Element: "Without consent of all parties" - California is a two-party consent state, meaning BOTH the website AND the user must consent
  • Penalty: Criminal misdemeanor, fine up to $2,500, civil liability

Legal Argument: Session recording is functionally equivalent to wiretapping. When you type a message and delete it before sending, you've chosen NOT to communicate it‚Äîbut the session recorder captured it anyway. This is interception of communications that were never transmitted.

üá∫üá∏ California Penal Code ¬ß 638.51 (TRAP AND TRACE)

Applies: YES HIGH VIOLATION

  • The Law: Prohibits installing or using a pen register or trap and trace device without consent to record addressing, routing, or signaling information
  • Why It Applies: Session recorders track which pages you visit, which elements you interact with, your navigation path‚Äîthis is "addressing" information
  • Requirement: Must obtain consent before installation

üá™üá∫ GDPR (European Union)

Requires Consent: YES CRITICAL

  • Article 6: Processing personal data requires a legal basis; consent is required for non-essential processing
  • Article 5(1)(a): Processing must be lawful, fair, and transparent
  • Recital 32: Consent should be given by a clear affirmative action
  • Why It Applies: Session recordings capture extensive personal data about behavior, preferences, and interactions

GDPR Requirements: Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous. Pre-ticked boxes are not valid. Users must be able to withdraw consent easily.

üá∫üá∏ CCPA/CPRA (California)

Requires Notice/Opt-Out: YES HIGH

  • Definition: Session recording data is "personal information" under CCPA
  • Sale/Sharing: If session recordings are shared with the recording service (they are), this may constitute a "sale" or "sharing"
  • Requirement: Must provide notice and "Do Not Sell My Personal Information" opt-out
  • Sensitive Data: If recordings capture passwords, SSNs, or financial data, this is "sensitive personal information" requiring additional protections

üö´ Do Cookie Consent Popups Block Session Recorders?

Answer: Usually NO

What Typically Happens:

  • Session recorder script loads immediately with the page
  • Recording begins before consent popup appears
  • Even if user rejects cookies, session recording often continues
  • Consent popups rarely mention session recording specifically
  • No controls to disable recording separate from cookies

Legal Problem:

Most implementations violate consent requirements by recording first and asking later (if at all). The recording has already captured your actions by the time you can respond to the consent popup.

üîç Why Session Recorders Are Especially Problematic

The Severity of Session Recording

  • Captures Deleted Content: If you type a message and delete it, the recording captured what you explicitly chose NOT to send
  • Reveals Hesitation: How long you hover over "Cancel" vs "Buy Now" reveals your decision-making process
  • Tracks Private Behavior: Where your mouse wanders when you think no one is watching
  • Potential for Sensitive Data: May capture passwords, credit cards, health information if not properly configured
  • Human Review: Real people at companies can watch replays of your session like a surveillance video
  • Permanent Record: Recordings are stored indefinitely for analysis
Legal Theory:

Session recording is qualitatively different from cookies or analytics. It's not just counting pageviews‚Äîit's creating a surveillance video of your online behavior. Courts and regulators should treat it like wiretapping, which has strong consent requirements.

‚úÖ What Proper Consent Would Look Like

Required Elements for Legal Consent:

  • Before Recording Starts: Consent must be obtained BEFORE any recording begins
  • Plain Language: "We will record your mouse movements, clicks, scrolling, and keyboard input on this website"
  • Purpose: "These recordings are reviewed by our employees and used for analytics and marketing"
  • Retention: "Recordings are kept for [X days/months]"
  • Opt-in: Recording only occurs AFTER explicit yes (not pre-checked box)
  • Easy opt-out: Clear way to stop recording at any time
  • No penalty: Denying consent doesn't block site access

Pixel Events (Facebook Pixel, Google Analytics)

Real-time behavior tracking and transmission to third parties

What Are Pixel Events?

Pixel events are JavaScript tracking codes that send detailed information about user actions to third-party servers (Facebook, Google, etc.) in real-time. Common events include: PageView (every page you visit), ViewContent (products/content you look at), AddToCart (items you consider buying), InitiateCheckout (starting purchase process), Purchase (completed transactions), Search (what you search for), Lead (form submissions).

// Facebook Pixel Example fbq('track', 'ViewContent', { content_name: 'Red Shoes', content_category: 'Footwear', content_ids: ['SKU123'], value: 99.99, currency: 'USD' }); // This sends to Facebook: // - What you viewed // - When you viewed it // - Your Facebook tracking ID // - Your IP address // - Device fingerprint

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Analysis: Do Pixel Events Require Consent?

YES - Consent Required (with nuances)

Pixel events almost certainly require consent, especially when transmitting data to third parties like Facebook. The legal case is strong but slightly less clear-cut than session recorders.

üá∫üá∏ California Penal Code ¬ß 631 (WIRETAP)

Applies: PROBABLY YES HIGH VIOLATION

  • The Law: Prohibits intercepting electronic communications without consent
  • Why It Applies: Pixel events intercept your browsing activity and transmit it to third parties (Facebook, Google) in real-time
  • Key Factor: The transmission happens as you browse, intercepting your "communication" with the website and routing it to Facebook
  • Counterargument: Could argue this is "addressing information" rather than "content," but the line is blurry

Legal Argument: When you browse a website, you're communicating with that website's servers. Facebook Pixel intercepts this communication stream and sends copies to Facebook without your consent‚Äîthat's wiretapping.

üá∫üá∏ California Penal Code ¬ß 638.51 (TRAP AND TRACE)

Applies: YES HIGH VIOLATION

  • The Law: Requires consent before installing devices that record addressing information
  • Why It Applies: Pixel events track which pages you visit, what you look at, your navigation path‚Äîclassic addressing information
  • Clear Violation: This statute seems tailor-made for pixel tracking

üá™üá∫ GDPR (European Union)

Requires Consent: YES CRITICAL

  • Article 6: Requires legal basis (consent) for processing personal data
  • ePrivacy Directive: Requires consent for storing/accessing information on devices
  • Why It Applies: Pixel events collect personal data (browsing behavior) and often set cookies
  • Third-Party Sharing: Sending data to Facebook/Google requires explicit consent under GDPR

CJEU Precedent: The Court of Justice of the European Union has ruled that Facebook "Like" buttons require consent. Pixel events are more invasive, so clearly require consent.

üá∫üá∏ CCPA/CPRA (California)

Requires Notice/Opt-Out: YES HIGH

  • Sale of Personal Information: Sending browsing data to Facebook/Google likely constitutes a "sale" under CCPA
  • Sharing: Even if not technically a "sale," it's definitely "sharing" personal information with third parties
  • Requirement: Must provide prominent "Do Not Sell My Personal Information" link and honor opt-outs
  • Disclosure: Must disclose categories of personal information shared and with whom

üö´ Do Cookie Consent Popups Block Pixel Events?

Answer: Usually NO

What Typically Happens:

  • Facebook Pixel loads immediately when page loads
  • PageView event fires automatically before consent popup
  • Even with cookies rejected, pixel events often continue (using fingerprinting instead)
  • Consent popups may mention "advertising cookies" but not "Facebook Pixel"
  • Users don't understand that Facebook is watching them on third-party sites

Example Timeline:

0ms: User visits Lowe's website 100ms: Facebook Pixel loads and fires PageView event 200ms: User clicks on drill ‚Üí ViewContent event fires 1500ms: Cookie consent popup appears 2000ms: User clicks "Reject All Cookies" Result: Facebook already received 2+ events about your shopping

üîç Why Pixel Events Are Problematic

Privacy Concerns with Pixel Tracking

  • Third-Party Surveillance: Facebook/Google track you across millions of websites, building comprehensive profiles
  • Shadow Profiles: They track you even if you don't have accounts
  • Opaque Data Use: You never know exactly what Facebook does with this data
  • Cross-Device Tracking: Links your behavior across phones, tablets, computers
  • Sold to Advertisers: Your data is monetized without your knowledge
  • Political Targeting: Can be used for voter manipulation
  • Discrimination: Can enable housing, employment, credit discrimination
The Surveillance Partnership:

Pixel events represent a coordinated surveillance arrangement between websites and tech giants. Lowe's lets Facebook watch their customers. Facebook provides free tracking in exchange for data. Neither fully discloses this to users. Both profit from the arrangement. Users are the product, not the customer.

Keyloggers & Keystroke Monitoring

Capturing what you type, including sensitive information

What Are Keyloggers?

Keyloggers are scripts that monitor and record keyboard input on websites. They capture: individual keystrokes (character-by-character), form field inputs (what you type in forms), timing between keystrokes (typing patterns), corrections/deletions (what you typed then erased), password fields (if not properly configured to exclude), search queries, credit card numbers.

// Keylogger implementation document.addEventListener('keydown', function(event) { // Captures every key you press var keyData = { key: event.key, timestamp: Date.now(), field: event.target.id, page: window.location.href }; // Sends to server immediately sendToTrackingServer(keyData); });

Common in: Some session recorders, form analytics tools (Hotjar Form Analysis), customer experience tools, fraud detection systems (though these should mask sensitive data).

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Analysis: Do Keyloggers Require Consent?

YES - ABSOLUTELY REQUIRES CONSENT

Keyloggers have the strongest legal requirement for consent. This is the most invasive form of tracking and almost certainly illegal without explicit consent.

üá∫üá∏ California Penal Code ¬ß 631 (WIRETAP)

Applies: DEFINITELY YES CRITICAL VIOLATION

  • The Law: Prohibits intercepting electronic communications without consent
  • Why It Clearly Applies: Keyloggers literally intercept what you're typing before you even submit it
  • Classic Wiretap: This is the textbook definition of wiretapping‚Äîintercepting communications as they're being created
  • Criminal Penalty: Could be charged as a criminal offense
  • Civil Liability: $5,000 per violation plus actual damages

Legal Argument: If you type "I'm interested in bankruptcy lawyers" in a search box but then delete it and search for something else, you explicitly chose NOT to communicate that query. The keylogger intercepted a communication you withheld. That's wiretapping.

üá∫üá∏ Federal Wiretap Act (18 U.S.C. ¬ß 2511)

Applies: YES FEDERAL CRIME

  • The Law: Federal law prohibiting interception of electronic communications
  • Penalty: Up to 5 years in prison, fines, civil liability
  • Why It Applies: Keylogging is electronic surveillance, clearly covered
  • Exception: One-party consent states allow recording with one party's consent, but users aren't consenting to keystroke capture

üá∫üá∏ Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)

May Apply: POTENTIAL

  • 18 U.S.C. ¬ß 1030(a)(2): Prohibits intentionally accessing a computer without authorization and obtaining information
  • Argument: Users authorize website interaction, but don't authorize keystroke monitoring‚Äîthat could be "without authorization"
  • Controversial: Courts split on whether violating ToS = unauthorized access

üá™üá∫ GDPR (European Union)

Requires Consent: ABSOLUTELY YES CRITICAL

  • Especially Invasive: Keystroke data is highly personal and sensitive
  • Lawfulness Principle: Processing must be lawful, fair, transparent‚Äîsecret keylogging is none of these
  • Data Minimization: Article 5(1)(c) requires collecting only necessary data‚Äîkeystroke logging is rarely necessary
  • Consent Requirements: Must be freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous

üá∫üá∏ CCPA/CPRA (California)

Requires Notice: YES HIGH

  • Sensitive Personal Information: Keystrokes may reveal passwords, SSNs, account credentials‚Äîthis is "sensitive" under CPRA
  • Enhanced Requirements: Sensitive PI has additional notice and opt-out requirements
  • Disclosure: Must clearly disclose keystroke monitoring in privacy policy

üö´ Do Cookie Consent Popups Block Keyloggers?

Answer: NO - And That's a Serious Problem

What Typically Happens:

  • Keylogging scripts load with page, before any consent
  • Start monitoring immediately as soon as you type anything
  • Cookie consent popups never mention keystroke monitoring
  • No separate control to disable keylogging
  • Operates completely invisibly‚Äîusers have no idea

Legal Problem:

Keylogging without consent is likely criminal wiretapping under both state and federal law. The fact that cookie popups don't address it doesn't make it legal‚Äîit makes the consent mechanism inadequate and potentially fraudulent ("we value your privacy" while secretly keylogging).

üîç Why Keyloggers Are Extremely Problematic

The Extreme Invasiveness of Keylogging

  • Passwords & Credentials: Can capture login information if not properly masked (many keyloggers don't mask adequately)
  • Financial Data: Credit card numbers, bank account information, SSNs
  • Health Information: Medical queries, symptoms, medication searches‚ÄîHIPAA implications
  • Private Thoughts: Captures what you write then delete‚Äîthoughts you explicitly chose not to share
  • Intimate Details: Personal messages, relationship issues, private concerns
  • Malicious Intent Detection: Some systems flag "suspicious" searches (like "how to cancel" or "competitor products")
  • No Reasonable Expectation: Users don't expect websites to be keylogging them like malware would
Why This is Different:

Keylogging crosses a bright red line. Cookies track which pages you visit. Session recorders watch what you click. But keyloggers intercept your actual thoughts as you type them‚Äîincluding thoughts you choose to keep private by deleting. This is fundamentally different and far more invasive than other tracking. It should be treated as criminal wiretapping.

‚úÖ What Proper Consent Would Look Like

Absolute Requirements for Legal Keylogging:

  • Explicit Disclosure: "We will record every keystroke you type on this website, including text you delete before submitting"
  • Sensitive Data Warning: "This may include passwords, credit cards, and other sensitive information"
  • Purpose Statement: "This data will be used for analytics and reviewed by our employees"
  • Opt-In Only: Must explicitly click "I consent to keystroke monitoring" (not bundled with other consents)
  • Easy Opt-Out: Clear toggle to disable keystroke monitoring
  • No Penalty: Must be able to use site without keystroke monitoring
  • Data Security: Must disclose how keystroke data is secured and when deleted

Reality Check:

No major website provides this level of disclosure for keylogging because if they did, almost no one would consent. The practice persists because it's hidden and users don't know it's happening.

Summary Findings:

All three tracking methods (session recorders, pixel events, keyloggers) almost certainly require explicit consent under California and EU law. However, most implementations violate these requirements by: (1) Operating before consent is obtained, (2) Continuing even after cookie rejection, (3) Not being mentioned in consent popups, (4) Having no separate controls to disable them.

Keyloggers are the most severe violation, potentially rising to criminal wiretapping. Session recorders are a close second. Pixel events are also serious, especially given the third-party surveillance implications.

üî® Enforcement Reality

Why These Violations Persist

Despite clear legal problems, these tracking methods are widespread because:

  • Rare Enforcement: Regulators haven't prioritized these issues yet
  • Technical Complexity: Hard for regulators to detect and understand
  • Industry Lobbying: Tech companies fight to keep these tools unregulated
  • User Ignorance: Most users don't know these tracking methods exist
  • High Value: Too profitable to abandon voluntarily
  • Settlement Culture: When caught, companies settle without admitting wrongdoing

Trend:

Enforcement is increasing. Recent years have seen more regulatory attention to session recording (especially in EU) and third-party tracking pixels. Keyloggers remain largely under the radar, but this may change as awareness grows.

üìã For Privacy Investigators

What to Document in Privacy Assessments

  • Session Recorders:
    • Which service is used (FullStory, Hotjar, etc.)
    • Whether recording starts before consent
    • Whether consent popup mentions session recording
    • Whether recording continues after cookie rejection
    • Whether sensitive fields are masked (passwords, SSNs)
  • Pixel Events:
    • Which pixels are present (Facebook, Google, etc.)
    • What events are being tracked
    • Whether events fire before consent
    • What data is transmitted (view Chrome DevTools Network tab)
    • Whether privacy policy discloses third-party sharing
  • Keyloggers:
    • Whether keystroke monitoring is present
    • Which fields are monitored (all? or specific forms?)
    • Whether sensitive fields are excluded
    • Whether any disclosure exists
    • Whether data is shared with third parties
Legal Theory for Claims:

These tracking methods are not "edge cases" of privacy law‚Äîthey're core violations of wiretapping statutes. When documenting privacy violations, emphasize that these aren't just GDPR or CCPA issues. They potentially violate criminal wiretap laws (PC ¬ß 631, 18 USC ¬ß 2511) and civil statutes with statutory damages (PC ¬ß 637.2, 18 USC ¬ß 2520). This elevates them above typical "cookie violations."
