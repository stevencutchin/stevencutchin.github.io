üî¶ Why "Additive"? Screens emit light, so colors ADD together. Red light + Green light = Yellow light. This is different from paint, where pigments SUBTRACT light (subtractive color). With light, mixing all colors gives you white (all wavelengths). With paint, mixing all colors gives you muddy brown (no wavelengths reflected).

üé≠ When Do We Need Alpha? Alpha is crucial for compositing images, creating UI elements with transparent backgrounds, antialiasing (smooth edges), soft shadows, particle effects, and any situation where pixels need to blend with what's behind them. Without alpha, every image would be rectangular with hard edges!

üìã Summary

üéØ Why RGB + Alpha?
  • R, G, B directly match the three types of color receptors in human eyes
  • Additive mixing of light is how screens produce colors (not pigments)
  • 8 bits per channel (256 levels) provides smooth gradations imperceptible to humans
  • 16.7 million colors (256¬≥) covers the full range of human-visible colors
  • Alpha channel enables transparency, compositing, and smooth blending
  • 32-bit RGBA (4 bytes per pixel) is efficient and hardware-optimized
  • Industry standard across all graphics hardware, software, and displays
üåà Fun Fact: The RGB color model was invented in the 1860s by James Clerk Maxwell, but it took until the 1950s for color TV to make it practical, and the 1980s for computer graphics to make it ubiquitous. Today, every pixel on every screen in the world uses RGB!
