Understanding the visible color space and device limitations

üìä Understanding Color Gamuts

A color gamut is the range of colors that can be reproduced by a device or color space. The CIE 1931 chromaticity diagram represents all colors visible to the human eye. Different display technologies and standards can only reproduce a subset of these visible colors, shown as triangular or polygonal regions on the diagram.

üìê Understanding the x and y Coordinates:

The CIE 1931 chromaticity diagram uses x and y coordinates that are derived from the tristimulus values X, Y, and Z:

x = X / (X + Y + Z)

y = Y / (X + Y + Z)

z = Z / (X + Y + Z) = 1 - x - y

These are normalized chromaticity coordinates that represent color independent of brightness. The coordinates always sum to 1, which is why we only need x and y (z is redundant). The x-axis represents the proportion of red-like sensation, while the y-axis represents the proportion of green-like sensation. Together they define the hue and saturation of a color, regardless of how bright or dim it is.

üí° Key Concept: The horseshoe-shaped region on the CIE diagram represents all colors visible to humans (the "spectral locus"). Display gamuts are triangles within this space, with vertices representing the primary colors (red, green, blue) that the display can produce. The area of overlap between gamuts shows which colors different devices can reproduce in common.

üí° Practical Implications

Understanding Gamut Limitations

üéØ What This Means for Your Work:

Color Clipping: When you work in a wide gamut space (like Adobe RGB) but display on sRGB, colors outside the sRGB triangle get "clipped" - they map to the nearest reproducible color on the gamut boundary. This means vivid greens, cyans, and some reds lose their vibrancy.

Device Dependency: The same image file looks different on different displays because each device can only reproduce colors within its gamut. A photo edited on a wide-gamut monitor (P3) may look dull on a standard sRGB laptop.

Common Pitfalls and Solutions

üö´ Common Mistakes:
  • Editing in sRGB, printing in Adobe RGB: Your prints will look dull because you didn't use the full color range available. Solution: Edit in Adobe RGB, convert to printer profile.
  • Sharing Adobe RGB images on web: Images appear oversaturated on sRGB displays without proper conversion. Solution: Always convert to sRGB before uploading.
  • Ignoring color profiles: Not embedding color profiles leads to inconsistent appearance. Solution: Always embed ICC profiles in images.
  • Assuming all displays are equal: What looks great on your wide-gamut monitor looks dull on client's standard display. Solution: Preview on sRGB for web work.
‚úÖ Best Practices:
  • Work in a wide gamut, deliver in target gamut: Edit in Adobe RGB or ProPhoto to preserve information, then convert to sRGB for web or appropriate profile for print
  • Use color management: Enable color management in your software and use ICC profiles correctly
  • Soft-proof before delivery: Preview how images will look in the target gamut before finalizing
  • Embed profiles: Always embed ICC profiles so other applications know how to interpret colors
  • Test on target devices: View your work on actual devices your audience will use
  • Consider gamut mapping: Use perceptual or relative colorimetric rendering intents when converting between gamuts

üî¨ Technical Details

About the CIE 1931 Diagram

The CIE 1931 xy chromaticity diagram was created by the International Commission on Illumination (CIE) based on experiments measuring human color perception. The horseshoe-shaped region represents all colors visible to the "standard observer" under standard illumination conditions.

üìê Understanding the Coordinates:

The x and y coordinates represent the chromaticity (hue and saturation) of colors, independent of brightness. Each point on the diagram represents a specific color:

  • Spectral locus (curved edge): Pure monochromatic light at specific wavelengths (380nm-700nm)
  • Purple line (straight bottom edge): Non-spectral purples (mixtures of red and blue)
  • White point (center region): Achromatic colors (gray/white) - D65 is daylight at 6500K
  • Distance from white point: Represents saturation (further = more saturated)
  • Angle from white point: Represents hue (which color)

Why Displays Use Triangles

Display gamuts form triangles because displays use three primary colors (RGB) to create all other colors through additive mixing. Any color within the triangle can be produced by mixing the three primaries at the vertices. Colors outside the triangle cannot be physically reproduced by that display. This is a fundamental limitation of trichromatic displays - we can never display all visible colors with just three primaries.

üîÆ The Future: Beyond Three Primaries

Research is ongoing into multi-primary displays (4, 5, or even 6 primaries) that would form larger polygons on the CIE diagram, covering more of the visible spectrum. However, these face significant technical and cost challenges. The Rec. 2020 standard was designed to cover ~75% of visible colors and represents an achievable target for future display technology.

Imaginary Colors in ProPhoto RGB

ProPhoto RGB is unique because some of its gamut extends outside the visible spectrum (horseshoe). These are called "imaginary colors" - they don't physically exist but are mathematically defined in the color space. This allows ProPhoto to encompass essentially all visible colors plus provide mathematical headroom, making it ideal for archival and extensive color grading. However, you must be careful when converting to smaller gamuts to avoid severe clipping.

üìã Summary

üéì Key Takeaways:
  • No display shows all colors: Even the widest gamut displays (Rec. 2020) only show ~75% of visible colors
  • sRGB is the common denominator: For web and general use, sRGB ensures your work looks consistent everywhere
  • Wide gamuts are for capture and editing: Use Adobe RGB or ProPhoto for photography workflow, then convert to sRGB for sharing
  • P3 is the new standard for premium devices: Modern iPhones, iPads, and MacBooks support the wider P3 gamut
  • Rec. 2020 is the future: UHDTV and HDR standards target this gamut, but current displays only partially support it
  • Color management matters: Always use proper color profiles and soft-proofing to ensure accurate color reproduction
  • Test on target devices: Colors look different on different displays - always check on the actual viewing device when possible
