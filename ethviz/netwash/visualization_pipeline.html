Reference Model · Information Visualization

How raw data becomes a picture you can explore

A visualization pipeline is the sequence of transforms that carries values from a source all the way to marks on a screen — and back again, as the person looking at it asks new questions. Here are the eight stages, in order.

pipeline.run() — raw values → embedded → glyphs → color → rendered
8 Stages

From source to sight

01ACQUIRE

Extract the data

Pull the raw values out of wherever they live — a file, a database, an API, a sensor stream — and bring them into reach.

Haber & McNabb: data input · Fry: acquire
02PARSE

Convert to a processing format

Parse and normalize the input into the pipeline's internal representation — clean records, typed fields, a table the later stages can rely on.

Card et al.: data tables · Fry: parse
03ANALYZE

Process and annotate

Run the analysis: aggregate, summarize, cluster, reduce dimensions, fit models — then attach the derived results back onto the records as new attributes.

Fry: filter / mine · Chi: analytical abstraction
04EMBED

Embed in a coordinate system

Map each record to a position in space — usually a 2D plane. Scales, projections, and layouts decide where every value lands.

Card et al.: spatial substrate · Munzner: encode
05MARK

Assign glyphs and geometry

Choose the marks — points, bars, lines, areas — and bind data attributes to their geometric channels: position, length, size, shape.

Bertin: marks & variables · Card et al.: visual structures
06COLOR

Assign color

Map an attribute onto a color scale — sequential, diverging, or categorical — to carry an extra channel of meaning the eye reads instantly.

Bertin: retinal variable · sequential / diverging scales
07RENDER

Load and display

Hand the encoded scene to the visualizer and draw it — rasterized to pixels or composited as vectors — into the view the person sees.

Haber & McNabb: rendering · Card et al.: views
08EXPLORE

Interact and explore

The person drives: zoom, pan, scan, and filter to move from overview to detail-on-demand, testing the questions the static picture can't answer alone.

Shneiderman: overview, zoom & filter, details-on-demand
The pipeline is a loop, not a line. Every interaction in stage 8 can feed back upstream — a filter changes the analysis, a zoom re-embeds the coordinates, a brushed selection re-colors the marks. Treating the human in the loop as part of the system, not its endpoint, is what separates a visualization from a static chart.
