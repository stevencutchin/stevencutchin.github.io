Imaging Sensors: Cameras & Depth

★ TRICORDER

Visual scanning for life signs. Records visual data. Spectral analysis of materials.

★ YOUR PHONE

1. Camera Sensor Basics

1.1 The Image Sensor

At the heart of every phone camera is a CMOS image sensor — a chip containing millions of photosites (pixels) arranged in a grid. Each photosite is a photodiode that converts incoming photons into an electrical charge. The charge is proportional to light intensity. A color filter array (typically a Bayer pattern of red, green, and green, and blue filters) sits over the photosites so each pixel captures one color channel. Software then interpolates the missing channels in a process called demosaicing.

Figure 5.1 — Bayer pattern color filter array. Each pixel captures one color; demosaicing reconstructs the full-color image.

1.2 Multi-Lens Systems

Modern flagships have 2–4 rear cameras with different focal lengths: an ultra-wide (13mm equivalent, ~120° field of view), a wide/main (24–26mm, the default camera), and optionally a telephoto (70–120mm for optical zoom). Rather than a complex zoom lens mechanism, the phone switches between fixed lenses and uses digital processing to blend the transitions.

2. Computational Photography

The defining revolution in phone cameras is not the hardware — it is the software. Computational photography uses the phone’s powerful neural processing unit (NPU) to combine multiple exposures, apply machine learning models, and produce images that far exceed what the tiny sensor could capture in a single frame.

Key techniques include HDR+ (High Dynamic Range), which captures a burst of frames at different exposures and merges them to preserve detail in both shadows and highlights; Night Mode, which takes long multi-second exposures with software stabilization to produce bright images in near-darkness; and Portrait Mode, which uses depth data (from dual cameras or LiDAR) to simulate shallow depth-of-field bokeh.

3. LiDAR and Time-of-Flight (ToF) Depth Sensors

3.1 How LiDAR Works

LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) projects thousands of infrared laser dots into the scene and measures how long each dot takes to bounce back. The round-trip time, at the speed of light, gives the distance to each point. This creates a 3D point cloud — a depth map of the environment.

Apple’s LiDAR scanner (available on Pro iPhones and iPads) can measure distances up to 5 meters with centimeter accuracy. Time-of-Flight (ToF) sensors on Android phones work similarly but typically with lower resolution.

AR Connection: LiDAR is what makes augmented reality on phones practical. It gives the phone real-time understanding of the 3D geometry of a room — where the floor is, where walls are, the shape of furniture. Without depth sensing, AR objects float unrealistically. With it, a virtual object can sit on a real table and be occluded by a real lamp.
