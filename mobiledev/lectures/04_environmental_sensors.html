Environmental Sensors: Light, Pressure & Proximity

★ TRICORDER

Analyzes atmospheric pressure, light levels, and radiation. Detects environmental conditions for crew safety assessment.

★ YOUR PHONE

Barometer measures atmospheric pressure (and estimates altitude). Ambient light sensor adjusts screen brightness. Proximity sensor detects nearby objects to turn off the screen during calls.

1. The Barometer

1.1 What It Measures

The barometer measures atmospheric pressure in hectopascals (hPa). Standard sea-level pressure is about 1013.25 hPa. Pressure decreases with altitude at roughly 12 hPa per 100 meters near sea level, which means the barometer can estimate relative altitude changes with surprising precision — down to about 0.3 meters.

1.2 How It Works

Phone barometers use a MEMS pressure sensor: a sealed chamber with a flexible membrane. Atmospheric pressure deflects the membrane, and piezoresistive elements on the membrane change resistance proportionally. The chip measures this resistance change and converts it to a pressure reading.

Figure 4.1 — MEMS barometer. Atmospheric pressure deflects a membrane over a sealed reference chamber. Piezoresistive elements convert deflection to an electrical signal.
Why Altitude Matters: GPS gives good horizontal position but mediocre altitude (±10–30 meters). The barometer provides much better relative altitude — it can tell which floor of a building you are on. This is how Apple Maps and Google Maps provide indoor floor detection. The catch: barometric altitude is relative and changes with weather. It must be calibrated against a known reference to give absolute altitude.

2. The Ambient Light Sensor

2.1 What It Measures

The ambient light sensor measures the brightness of the surrounding environment in lux. A dark room might read 10–50 lux, an office 300–500 lux, and direct sunlight 50,000–100,000 lux. The primary use is auto-brightness — the phone dims the screen in dark rooms and brightens it in sunlight.

2.2 How It Works

The sensor is a photodiode — a semiconductor that generates a current proportional to the amount of light hitting it. Modern sensors include filters to approximate the human eye’s sensitivity curve (the photopic response), so the lux reading corresponds to perceived brightness rather than raw photon count. On newer iPhones, the ambient light sensor is integrated directly under the display, eliminating the need for a visible notch or cutout.

3. The Proximity Sensor

3.1 What It Measures

The proximity sensor detects whether an object (typically your face) is close to the screen. It returns a simple binary signal: near or far. Its primary purpose is to turn off the screen during phone calls so your cheek does not accidentally press buttons.

3.2 How It Works

Most phones use an infrared emitter/detector pair. The emitter sends out an infrared beam. When an object is nearby, the beam bounces back and is detected by the receiver. The sensor does not measure distance precisely — it simply triggers when the reflected signal exceeds a threshold, typically at a range of about 1–5 centimeters.

Figure 4.2 — Proximity sensor using IR reflection. An infrared emitter sends a beam; if an object is within ~5cm, the reflected light triggers the detector.

4. As a Developer

Environmental sensors are mostly handled by the OS automatically — you rarely need to read the light sensor or proximity sensor directly. The barometer is the exception, accessible through expo-sensors:

import { Barometer } from 'expo-sensors';

Barometer.addListener(({ pressure, relativeAltitude }) => {
  console.log(`Pressure: ${pressure} hPa`);
  // relativeAltitude (iOS only): change in meters since listener started
});

The Invisible Sensors: These three sensors are the “silent workers” of your phone. You never think about them, but they are constantly active — adjusting brightness as you walk between rooms, detecting your ear during calls, and providing altitude data that helps navigation apps place you on the correct floor. Good engineering is invisible engineering.
