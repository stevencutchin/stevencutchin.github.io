Communication Radios: WiFi, Bluetooth, NFC & Cellular

★ TRICORDER

Communicates with the Enterprise over subspace frequencies. Short-range data link with other tricorders.

★ YOUR PHONE

5G/LTE cellular reaches towers kilometers away. WiFi 6E provides gigabit local networking. Bluetooth connects to earbuds and watches. NFC enables tap-to-pay. UWB provides centimeter-level spatial awareness.

1. Understanding Radio Range

Your phone contains 5–6 distinct radio transceivers, each optimized for a different range and data rate. They are not “sensors” in the traditional sense — they are communication devices — but they also function as sensors: WiFi signals reveal nearby access points (indoor positioning), Bluetooth beacons detect proximity, and cellular signals indicate which cell tower you are connected to.

Figure 6.1 — Approximate range comparison of phone radios (not to scale). All can operate concurrently.

2. WiFi

WiFi provides high-bandwidth local networking. Modern phones support WiFi 6E (802.11ax) which operates on 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz bands. Beyond internet connectivity, WiFi serves as a location sensor: the phone scans for nearby access points and uses their known positions (from databases maintained by Apple and Google) to estimate location indoors where GPS signals cannot reach.

3. Bluetooth and BLE

Bluetooth Classic handles high-bandwidth connections like audio streaming to earbuds. Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) is a separate protocol optimized for low power, used for smartwatch connections, fitness trackers, and beacon detection. BLE beacons (iBeacons, Eddystone) are small transmitters placed in physical locations that a phone can detect and use for indoor navigation, museum guides, or retail proximity notifications.

4. NFC (Near-Field Communication)

NFC operates at extremely short range (typically 1–4 cm) and is the technology behind contactless payments (Apple Pay, Google Pay). It works by inductive coupling — the phone’s NFC antenna generates a magnetic field that powers and communicates with a passive NFC tag or payment terminal. The short range is a security feature: it is physically difficult to intercept an NFC transaction from a distance.

5. Cellular (5G / LTE)

The cellular radio connects to carrier networks through base stations (cell towers). 5G operates in three bands: low-band (700–900 MHz, wide coverage), mid-band (2.5–3.7 GHz, balanced), and mmWave (24–39 GHz, extremely fast but very short range). The phone constantly monitors signal strength from multiple towers and hands off connections seamlessly as you move.

6. UWB (Ultra-Wideband)

UWB is the newest radio in phones. It uses very short pulses across a wide frequency range to measure precise distance and direction to other UWB devices — accurate to within 10 centimeters. This powers Apple’s AirTag spatial finding (the directional arrow), nearby AirDrop, and car key proximity detection. Think of it as “Bluetooth with spatial awareness.”

7. As a Developer

Most radio functionality is mediated by the OS. You do not directly control the WiFi or cellular radios. However, some APIs are available:

// Check network status
import * as Network from 'expo-network';
const state = await Network.getNetworkStateAsync();
console.log(state.type); // 'wifi', 'cellular', 'none'
console.log(state.isConnected); // true/false

// NFC and BLE require native modules
// beyond Expo's managed workflow
Battery and Data: While WiFi and Bluetooth consume moderate power, cellular radios — especially 5G — can drain battery significantly, particularly when signal is weak and the phone boosts transmit power to reach a distant tower. Apps that transfer large amounts of data should prefer WiFi and defer non-urgent transfers to WiFi connectivity.
