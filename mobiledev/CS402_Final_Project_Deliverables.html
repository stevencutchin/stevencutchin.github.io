Each Deliverable, Explained

1
Proposal
⭐ 5 points 📅 Due April 8 📄 2 pages
Commit to an idea before you start building the wrong thing.
Points
5
Due
April 8
Length
2 pages

The proposal is your team's first public commitment. Its job is to convince your instructor — and yourselves — that you have a real, buildable idea with a clear purpose. You don't need all the answers yet, but you do need to have thought through the core question: who is this for, and what problem does it solve?

What to include

  • App concept — what does it do, in plain language a friend could understand
  • Target user — who uses this and why they'd care
  • Core features — the 2–4 things it must do to be useful (not a wishlist)
  • Team members — names and rough role assignments
  • Why mobile? — a sentence on why a phone is the right form factor for this app

2
Design Document
⭐ 35 points 📅 Due April 21 📐 Written doc + wireframes
Show what the app will look like before a single line of code is written.
Points
35
Due
April 21
Format
Doc + wireframes

The design document is a blueprint for your user experience. You're answering the question: what will users actually see and do? Wireframes don't need to be beautiful — they need to be clear. A rectangle with the word "camera button" in it is fine. What matters is that someone could read this document and understand how users move through your app.

What to include

  • Screen inventory — list every screen the user will encounter
  • Wireframes — hand-drawn or digital, one per major screen
  • Navigation flow — how does a user get from screen A to screen B?
  • Key interactions — what happens when they tap, swipe, or submit?
  • Data displayed — what information does each screen show?
💡 Tip: Figma, Excalidraw, and even Google Slides all work fine for wireframes. Paper sketches scanned or photographed are completely acceptable. The medium doesn't matter; the thinking does.

3
Architecture Document
⭐ 35 points 📅 Due April 28 🏗️ Written doc + diagrams
Explain how the system is built — components, data, and the connections between them.
Points
35
Due
April 28
Format
Doc + diagrams

Where the design document describes what users see, the architecture document describes what the code does. You're working at the level of components, data structures, and external services — not individual lines of code. Think of it as the document a new developer on your team would read to understand how everything fits together.

What to include

  • Component hierarchy — what are your major React Native components and how do they relate?
  • Data model — what data does the app store? What shape does it take?
  • State management strategy — where does state live, and how does it flow?
  • External services — APIs, device sensors, storage — what and why
  • Navigation structure — how does React Navigation organize your screens?
Architecture ≠ Design. Your design document shows what the user sees. Your architecture document shows what the programmer built. A user doesn't care about state management. A developer doesn't need a wireframe. Keep them separate in your mind and on the page.

4
User Manual
⭐ 35 points 📅 Due May 4 📖 Written doc + screenshots
Write documentation your grandmother could follow — or your first-time user definitely will.
Points
35
Due
May 4
Format
Doc + screenshots

The user manual is the document you hand to someone who has never seen your app before. It explains how to install it, how to navigate it, and how to use every major feature. Every section should have a real screenshot from the actual running app — not a wireframe, not a mockup. If it's in your app, it should be in the manual.

What to include

  • Installation / setup — how do they get it running? (Expo Go QR code is fine)
  • Feature walkthroughs — step-by-step instructions for each major feature, with screenshots
  • Error states — what should a user do if something goes wrong?
  • Known limitations — be honest about what the app can't do yet
💡 Tip: Write the manual as if you won't be in the room. If a user can't figure out a feature from your instructions alone, the instructions need more detail. Real screenshots from your physical device make this document dramatically more useful.

5
Source Code + Full Package
⭐ 70 points 📅 Due May 4 📦 ZIP submission
The actual app. Working, readable, and ready for a stranger to clone and run.
Points
70
Due
May 4
Format
ZIP via Canvas

This is the centerpiece of the project — everything else documents what you built here. The grader will unzip your submission, run npm install, and launch it with Expo. It should work. Code quality matters: meaningful names, logical structure, and components that do one thing well go a long way.

What to include in your ZIP

  • Full Expo project — all source files, app.json, package.json
  • All four documents — Proposal, Design, Architecture, User Manual (PDF or HTML)
  • README.md — how to run your app in three commands or fewer
  • No node_modules — exclude the folder; we'll run npm install

Code is graded on

  • Does it run without errors on Expo Go?
  • Does it do what the proposal and design doc said it would?
  • Is the code organized into sensible components?
  • Are variable and function names meaningful?
  • Is there evidence of real testing (not just "I ran it once")?

6
Final Presentation
⭐ 35 points 📅 Due May 7 🎥 Video — recorded
Sell your app to someone who's never heard of it — in five minutes or less.
Points
35
Due
May 7
Format
Video (recorded)

Your presentation is a recorded video demo of your working app. Record from a real phone — not the simulator. Walk the viewer through what the app does, why it matters, and anything technically interesting you're proud of. This is the closest thing to an interview demo you'll do in this class. Practice it before you record it.

What to cover

  • Hook — what problem does this solve and who is it for? (30 seconds)
  • Feature demo — screen-record yourself actually using the app on your phone
  • Technical highlight — one thing under the hood you're proud of and why
  • Reflection — what would you do differently with more time?
💡 Phone preferred, simulator accepted. Recording from a physical device is ideal — it shows your app running in the real world, which is always the most convincing demo. That said, screen-recording from a simulator is completely fine if phone recording is giving you trouble. Either way, make sure the core features are visible and working in the video.

7
Peer Evaluation
⭐ 5 points 📅 Due May 7 📝 Individual form (Canvas)
Give your teammates honest, useful feedback — the kind that helps rather than stings.
Points
5
Due
May 7
Submitted
Individually

Each team member fills out a peer evaluation form individually on Canvas — your teammates won't see your individual responses, only aggregated feedback. The goal isn't to rate teammates on a scale from good to bad. It's to notice who took on which work, how you collaborated, and what you'd do differently in a future team.

What the form asks

  • How would you describe each teammate's contributions?
  • Were responsibilities distributed fairly?
  • What would have helped the team work better together?
  • What did you personally contribute that you're proud of?
💡 This is not a gotcha. It's a professional skill. Giving structured feedback on a colleague's work — specific, actionable, and kind — is something you will absolutely do in a real job. Practice it here.
8
Project Runs
⭐ 30 points 📅 May 7 📱 Live on device
The simplest 30 points in the course — your app just has to work on a real phone.
Points
30
When
Finals Week
How
On your phone

This is the clearest grading criteria in the course: can your instructor scan a QR code (or run your app via Expo) and have it launch and do what it's supposed to do on a physical device? This isn't a trick question. It's the whole point. Everything you've learned this semester — lifecycle, layout, navigation, data, sensors — comes down to this one moment.

What "runs" means

  • Launches without crashing on Expo Go
  • Core features work on a real device (not just the simulator)
  • Navigation works as described in your design document
  • Data persists or loads as expected
Test on a real device early and often. Bugs that don't appear in the simulator regularly appear on physical hardware — font scaling, platform permissions, keyboard behavior, and camera access all behave differently. Don't discover this for the first time on May 7.

The North Star

Walk into a job interview with a working app on your phone. Hand it to the interviewer. Let them play with it. That's the goal. Every deliverable in this project is practice for something real engineers do — proposing, designing, documenting, presenting, and shipping. The grade is just the scoreboard. The app is the prize.

Start from full credit. These deliverables are graded by looking for what's there, not hunting for what's missing. A complete, honest effort on each one will get you the points.
