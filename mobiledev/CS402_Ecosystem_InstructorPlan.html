📋 Instructor Lesson Plan

CS 402 — Mobile Application Development

The On-Phone Integrated Ecosystem
🕐 Total time: 75 minutes 📅 Semester: Spring 2026 🎓 Type: Conceptual / Discussion-heavy 📄 Student doc: CS402_OnPhone_Ecosystem.html 🔙 Prior class: On-Phone Integrated Ecosystem (reading) 🔜 Next class: Integrated Service Support

Lesson Overview

This is one of the most conceptually rich lectures in the course — and one of the least code-heavy. Unlike camera or sensors, which have concrete APIs to demonstrate, the ecosystem lecture asks students to think architecturally and ethically about the environment their apps inhabit. The central shift you're facilitating is from "how do I make this feature work" to "how does my app behave as a citizen among other apps."

Students at this stage of the course have built enough to feel the constraints firsthand — they've hit permission denials, seen their app get killed in the background, or wondered why a file disappeared. This lecture gives language and structure to experiences they've already had. Use that grounding actively: ask students to connect concepts to things they've already built.

The responsible development guidelines in Section 7 of the student doc often generate the strongest discussions of the semester. Budget generously for the notification and privacy segments — students have strong opinions and good real-world examples from apps they use daily.

Learning Objectives

Explain
Describe how a mobile OS allocates shared resources (CPU, RAM, battery, network) among multiple simultaneous apps.
Explain
Define the app sandbox model and articulate what it can and cannot access directly.
Trace
Walk through the mobile app lifecycle states and identify what code should run at each transition.
Contrast
Compare iOS and Android background execution policies and IPC mechanisms.
Analyse
Identify the threading model in a React Native app and explain why blocking the JS thread matters.
Evaluate
Apply the eight responsible development guidelines to critique real app behaviours and justify design decisions.

What Makes This Lecture Different

  • Low code, high concept. The few code references (AppState, useEffect cleanup) are familiar; the challenge is architecture and ethics, not syntax.
  • Discussion-driven. Segments 2, 6, and 7 work best as facilitated conversations, not lectures. Come with prompts, not monologues.
  • Students have lived this. Permission denials, app kills, battery drain — they've experienced all of these. Mine that experience throughout.
  • No demo device required. Unlike camera or sensors, all of today's content is explained through diagrams and discussion. Use the student doc SVGs projected on screen.
⚙️ Prep required before class: Read through the student document once and identify 2–3 apps on your own phone that you can use as concrete examples during the responsible development segment. A fitness app that requests "always-on" location when it only needs it during workouts, a news app with aggressive daily notification badges, or any app that asks for permissions on first launch — these make the guidelines tangible. Having specific named apps ready is much stronger than hypotheticals.

Hook and Framing — The Phone as a Shared City
Establishing the ecosystem metaphor that carries through the whole lecture
⏱ 7 min

Open with a concrete, personal question before showing any slides or diagrams. Students need to arrive at the ecosystem framing through their own experience before you name it.

💬 Opening cold call (2–3 students): "Open your phone's battery usage screen right now. What's draining it most? Is that the app you'd have expected? Now look at storage. Which app is taking the most space? Did you know that before just now?"

Give students 60 seconds to actually check their phones. This is unusual in a lecture setting and immediately creates engagement. Use what they find to launch the framing:

  • That battery screen is the OS giving you a ledger of who has been spending shared resources. The apps on that list had no idea they were being tracked or compared — and neither does yours, right now.
  • Everything we've built so far has been thought about in isolation — "make this feature work in my app." Today's shift: your app is one tenant in a building with dozens of other tenants, sharing walls, pipes, and electricity. How you behave affects everyone else.
  • Project the city SVG from the student document. Walk through it: the OS is the infrastructure — scheduler, memory manager, permission gate — not just a platform. It owns the ground everything runs on.
  • The three things to carry out of today: (1) what the sandbox gives you and limits you to, (2) how the OS manages your app's life cycle without asking, (3) what responsible citizenship looks like in this environment.
📝 Tone note: This lecture has a higher ratio of "thinking" to "doing" than any other in the course. Acknowledge that explicitly: "Today is less about code and more about mental models. That's intentional — the mental models are what let you make good engineering decisions automatically, without having to look everything up."
2
The App Sandbox — Your World Has Walls
Private storage, shared resources, permission gates, and the IPC ceiling
⏱ 10 min
🖊 Draw first, show diagram second
Before projecting Figure 2 from the student doc, sketch the sandbox on the board as a box within a box. Ask students what they think lives inside the inner box. Take 4–5 guesses, write them down, then reveal the diagram. Students who guessed correctly are more likely to retain the concept.

Talking Points

  • The sandbox is the OS's answer to the question: "how do we let thousands of apps run on one device without any of them being able to harm the user or each other?" Every app gets a private container. Files, memory, and preferences inside it are invisible to all other apps.
  • Walk through the storage table from Section 2 of the student doc. The distinction between temp cache (unreliable, OS can purge), documents directory (persistent), and secure storage (encrypted) maps directly to decisions students have been making without necessarily understanding the tradeoffs.
  • Shared resources — camera, GPS, microphone, photo library — live outside all sandboxes and are accessed only through the OS permission gate. The permission system isn't friction the OS added to annoy developers; it's the mechanism that makes sharing possible at all.
  • Direct access to another app's sandbox is impossible by design. The only path between apps is IPC through OS-controlled channels — covered in Segment 5.
Connect to prior experience: Ask: "When we used AsyncStorage earlier in the semester, where was that data stored? Could another app on the device read it?" The answer (no — private to your sandbox) should now make intuitive sense in the context of the diagram.
✅ Check for Understanding
  1. "Your app writes a JSON file to the documents directory. The user uninstalls and reinstalls the app. Is the file still there?"
    → No — uninstalling removes the sandbox entirely, including all documents. (iOS may offer backup via iCloud; Android may offer backup via Google Drive, but neither is guaranteed.)
  2. "You want to store a user's auth token securely. Which storage type from the table do you use, and why?"
    → Secure storage (Keychain on iOS, Keystore on Android). Encrypted by the OS, survives app updates, and is not accessible to other apps or backup systems.
3
The App Lifecycle — States Your App Passes Through
Active, Inactive, Background, Suspended, Terminated — and why you must handle all of them
⏱ 12 min

This segment often produces the strongest "aha" moments of the lecture, because students have experienced lifecycle transitions first-hand (app state lost after backgrounding, data missing after a crash) but didn't have a model for what was happening. Give the diagram time to land before moving to implications.

Talking Points

  • Project Figure 3 (the state machine). Walk through each state by name, but anchor each one to a physical gesture: Active = using the app. Inactive = notification drop-down pulled over it. Background = home button pressed. Suspended = been background for a while, frozen in RAM. Terminated = OS needed that RAM for something else.
  • Critical framing: the OS transitions your app between these states without asking, and without warning. There is no "are you ready to go to background?" handshake. You either handle the transition in your code, or your users lose data.
  • The key developer responsibility: save any unsaved state before the transition, not after. Once your app is in background, the OS may kill it with no further code execution. If a user has a half-filled form and presses the home button, that data is gone unless you persisted it on the 'background' AppState event.
  • Introduce AppState API briefly — not a deep dive, just the concept. The important thing is that students know it exists and that background detection is a two-line hook, not an architecture problem.
  • Contrast iOS and Android briefly using the platform comparison from the student doc. iOS is more aggressive about suspending; Android gives more background latitude but throttles it through battery optimization. Neither platform owes your app continuous execution time.
📱 Live demonstration — show lifecycle in action
Open any running app on your phone. Press home. Open another app. Come back. Ask: "Did that app 'keep running' while you were away?" Answer: it depends on the OS, battery state, and how much RAM other apps needed. This is the most concrete possible illustration of why lifecycle handling is non-optional.
📝 Connect to past pain: Ask the class: "Has anyone had an app lose their data — a form they were filling in, a message they were composing — when they switched to another app and came back?" Most hands will go up. That's a lifecycle bug. The developer either didn't listen for the background event, or listened but didn't save. Name it as an engineering failure, not bad luck.
✅ Check for Understanding
  1. "A user is filling out a multi-step form in your app. They get a phone call. When they return, the form is empty. What went wrong?"
    → The app was killed in the background while the call was happening. The developer didn't persist form state on the 'background' AppState event. Should have saved to AsyncStorage or state management when transitioning away.
  2. "In the lifecycle diagram, which transition is the one you can never code a response to — the one that just happens?"
    → The Suspended → Terminated transition. The OS evicts suspended apps from RAM silently. There is no callback for this. You must have already saved everything before suspension.
4
Shared Resource Competition
CPU, RAM, battery, network, storage — and how misuse shows up to users
⏱ 10 min

The resource competition segment is most effective as a series of concrete cause-and-effect explanations rather than abstract principles. Lead with the symptom students would observe, then work backwards to the cause.

Talking Points

  • Battery: "The user says your app drains their battery even when they're not using it." Cause: background GPS polling, a recurring network timer, or an audio session left open. The OS battery ledger will show your app. This is one of the top reasons users uninstall apps — and the App Store battery usage report surfaces it publicly in reviews.
  • RAM: Phones have no swap space. When RAM is full, the OS kills the least-recently-used suspended app — probably yours. Large image arrays, un-disposed video buffers, and circular references in event listeners are the most common causes. FlatList exists precisely because loading 500 full-resolution images into a ScrollView will exhaust available memory.
  • Network / Radio: Making a separate network call for every user keystroke (live search with no debounce) keeps the radio awake far longer than necessary. The cellular radio has a tail-time — it stays powered for seconds after a request completes, burning battery even if no data is moving. Batch requests, debounce inputs, and cache aggressively.
  • Project Figure 4 from the student doc. The battery bar showing 64% is intentionally alarming — walk through what could cause a single app to consume that share (constant GPS + network polling + screen kept awake).
  • Cover the iOS vs. Android background execution comparison from the diagram. iOS declares background modes in advance in app.json; undeclared apps get ~30 seconds to clean up then freeze. Android's WorkManager is the sanctioned way to do deferred work. Neither platform tolerates general background code execution in production builds.
💬 Discussion prompt: "You're building a fitness tracker. The user starts a run. Your app needs to track their GPS position every 10 seconds for the next 45 minutes — even if they lock the phone. How would you handle this on iOS? On Android? What are the tradeoffs?" Let students reason through it for 2 minutes before guiding toward the answer (iOS: location background mode declared in app.json; Android: Foreground Service with a persistent notification).
📝 Don't overload platform details here. Students don't need to memorize background mode declaration syntax today. The goal is awareness that background execution is gated, platform-specific, and requires explicit opt-in. When they need to implement it, they'll reference the docs. Right now they need to know it's not free.
5
Inter-App Communication
How apps talk across walls — deep links, Share Sheet, Intents, and Expo Linking
⏱ 8 min

This segment is shorter and more practical. Students understand the sandbox already; IPC is the answer to "ok, so how do apps actually share data with each other at all?" Keep it grounded in examples they've used as phone users.

Talking Points

  • Ask: "Has anyone tapped 'Share' in one app and had it open in another? Or tapped a link in an email that opened directly in the right app?" That's IPC. The OS brokered that handoff — neither app accessed the other's memory directly.
  • Project Figure 5. The key concept: the OS-mediated channel in the middle is not optional plumbing — it's the only channel. Apps communicate through the OS, never around it.
  • Deep links / URL schemes: Your app registers a scheme (e.g. myapp://) in app.json. When any app or website fires that URL, the OS routes it to your app. This is how "Open in App" buttons work in mobile browsers, and how push notifications can open a specific screen.
  • iOS Share Sheet vs. Android Intents: These are the two platform-specific channels worth naming. Share Sheet is the iOS bottom drawer that appears when you tap Share. Android Intents are a more general messaging system that can start activities, pass data to services, or broadcast events system-wide. Expo Linking abstracts both.
  • Expo abstraction: Linking.openURL() handles both platforms. Students don't need to write native intent code or configure Share Extensions for Project 4 — but they should know what's underneath the abstraction.
Practical connection for the final project: Deep links become relevant for any app that sends notifications (tapping the notification should open the right screen), handles OAuth redirects (the auth provider redirects back to your app URL), or integrates with other apps via Share. Plant this seed now — they'll revisit it.
✅ Check for Understanding
  1. "Your app displays a PDF. You want a 'Share' button that lets users open it in their PDF reader app of choice. What mechanism handles this?"
    → iOS Share Sheet / Android Intent with ACTION_VIEW. Expo's Sharing module abstracts this. The OS presents the list of compatible apps; your app never directly interacts with the destination app.
  2. "A user taps a push notification and lands on a specific post in your app. What technology made that routing possible?"
    → Deep links / URL schemes. The notification payload contains a URL like myapp://post/42; the OS routes it to your app which parses the path and navigates to the correct screen.
6
The React Native Runtime Environment
JS thread, Native thread, Bridge/JSI, and why blocking the JS thread freezes the UI
⏱ 7 min

Keep this segment tight. The threading model is important context, not the main event. Students need to understand why the "don't block the JS thread" rule exists, not become experts in the React Native bridge architecture.

Talking Points

  • Project Figure 6. React Native runs your JavaScript on a dedicated JS thread (Hermes engine on modern Expo). The native UI renders on a separate native thread. They communicate through the Bridge (or the newer JSI in Expo SDK 50+).
  • Because the JS thread handles both your business logic and the event loop that processes user touches, a long synchronous operation on the JS thread starves the event loop. The UI appears frozen. Buttons don't respond. The OS watchdog may flag the app as "not responding."
  • The practical rule: never run a loop that takes more than a few milliseconds synchronously on the main JS thread. Parsing a large JSON response, generating a thumbnail, sorting 10,000 records — all of these should be done asynchronously or moved off the main thread using InteractionManager.
  • Good news: fetch(), file I/O, and most Expo SDK calls are already async and dispatch to background threads. You get the result back via await without blocking. The danger is code you write yourself — big for loops, complex sort operations, synchronous JSON parsing on large payloads.
  • The native thread keeps running even when JS is busy. This is why Animated and Reanimated can run smooth animations while JS is doing other work — the animation runs natively, off the JS thread entirely.
⚠️ Common student mistake: Processing a large array (e.g. filtering 5,000 store locations) inside a useMemo with no throttling, called every render. Each render freezes the UI for however long the computation takes. The fix: memoize correctly (check dependencies), move heavy computation outside the render cycle, or use a web worker equivalent via Expo's task manager.
7
Responsible App Development — The Eight Guidelines
Battery, memory, privacy, network, notifications, storage, permissions, accessibility
⏱ 13 min

This is the segment with the highest discussion potential in the entire course. Don't lecture through all eight guidelines sequentially — that will lose the room. Instead, use a rotating discussion format: name the guideline, give a real-world example of an app that violates it, and ask students what the right behaviour would have been. Aim for student voices to carry at least half of this segment.

📝 Have specific apps ready. "A hypothetical app" is weak. "Snapchat's location tracking in 2017," "Facebook's continuous microphone access claims," "LinkedIn requesting contacts on first launch" — these are specific, remembered, and debatable. Students engage with named examples in a way they don't with abstractions. Keep examples professionally relevant: avoid anything that could read as personal criticism of a student's preferred app.

Guideline-by-Guideline Teaching Notes

GuidelineKey Teaching PointDiscussion Prompt / Example
🔋 Battery GPS + screen + network = the battery trifecta. Background polling is the silent killer. The battery usage screen in Settings is a public shaming ledger. "What's the worst battery-draining app on your phone right now? What do you think it's doing?" Students looked this up in Segment 1 — they have answers.
🧠 Memory useEffect cleanup is not optional. Subscriptions and listeners that aren't removed are the most common memory leak source in student code. FlatList over ScrollView for any list that could grow. "How many of you have returned a cleanup function from every useEffect that adds an event listener? How many haven't?" (honest show of hands — no judgment).
🔐 Privacy Data minimisation is a legal obligation in most markets (GDPR, CCPA), not just an ethical ideal. Every piece of user data stored is a liability: a breach vector, a regulatory risk, a trust debt. "Your recipe app. Do you need to store the user's full name, date of birth, and phone number to show them their saved recipes? What's the minimum?" Force students to articulate the minimum necessary set.
📡 Network Radio wake-on-request is expensive. Debounce search inputs. Cache API responses. Don't poll when push notifications or WebSockets would serve. Fail gracefully on slow connections — show a skeleton, not a frozen screen. "Have you ever built a search box that calls the API on every keystroke? What happened on a slow network?" Most students have done this. Don't shame — frame as a learning moment.
🔔 Notifications Permission is granted once and denied permanently if abused. The notification inbox is the most intimate digital space a user shares with an app. One irrelevant notification is one step toward "Allow Never." "Which app sends you the most notifications you actually find useful? Which sends the most you immediately dismiss? What's the difference in how they use notifications?" Students usually have strong opinions here.
💾 Storage Temp cache is not free. Undeleted download caches, uncompressed image originals, and accumulated log files fill devices slowly and invisibly. Give users a "Clear Cache" option. Set maximum sizes on downloaded content. "Check your phone right now: which app is using the most storage? Is that expected given what it does? What do you think it's storing?" (quick 30-second check, like Segment 1).
🔑 Permissions The moment of request and the quality of the explanation are the two most important variables. Requesting on launch = denied. Requesting with a clear why = granted. The app.json permission string is the first impression. "Think of a permission request you've seen in an app that made you immediately tap Allow vs. one you immediately denied. What was different about how they asked?" Very concrete discussion.
♿ Accessibility This is the only guideline that is also a legal requirement in many countries (ADA, EAA). VoiceOver / TalkBack users are real users. Every unlabelled button is a barrier. Test on real screen reader hardware before shipping. If possible, open your own app (or the student doc) in VoiceOver/TalkBack live and navigate through it. Hearing unlabelled elements read aloud as "button, button, image" is more persuasive than any diagram.
📝 Pacing note for this segment: You will not cover all eight in 13 minutes at discussion depth. Choose 3–4 that you have the strongest examples for and give them full treatment. Let the student doc cover the rest in reading. Battery, notifications, privacy, and permissions generate the most engagement and have the most direct connection to project work — prioritise those if you need to cut.
8
The Ecosystem Contract, Q&A, and Bridge to Integrated Service Support
Synthesising the lecture and setting up the next class
⏱ 8 min

Synthesising the Contract

Project Figure 8 from the student doc — the split-panel contract diagram. This is a strong closing image because it reframes everything covered today from a two-sided relationship rather than a list of rules.

  • The OS side of the contract — sandbox, lifecycle management, permission gate, IPC brokering, app store distribution — is real infrastructure that costs enormous engineering effort to provide. It's not free.
  • The developer side — resource respect, lifecycle hygiene, privacy minimisation, permission etiquette, accessibility — is the cost of entry. Apps that violate these terms get flagged in reviews, rejected from app stores, deleted by users, and increasingly throttled or penalised by OS-level controls.
  • The 2024 Apple automatic permission revocation example from the student doc is a good closer: the OS itself is now an active enforcer of ecosystem citizenship, not just a passive platform. The market and the platform are moving in the same direction.
💬 Exit question (cold call 1–2 students): "Name one concrete change you would make to an app you've already built in this course — based on what you learned today — to make it a better ecosystem citizen. It can be tiny." This surfaces understanding, generates accountability, and creates a natural bridge to project work.

Bridge to Next Class — Integrated Service Support

🔜 Next class: Integrated Service Support. Now that students understand the ecosystem environment their app lives in, the next lecture explores the ecosystem services it can connect to: push notifications, background sync, OAuth, analytics, and crash reporting. The mental model built today — OS as broker, permissions as gates, IPC as the only channel — is the foundation for all of those integrations.
📝 Reading suggestion: Recommend students review the student doc (CS402_OnPhone_Ecosystem.html) Sections 3 and 7 before next class. The lifecycle state machine and responsible development guidelines will come up again when discussing background sync and push notification design, and students who've internalised them will follow the next lecture much more easily.

Differentiation and Pacing Notes

If class is running ahead of schedule

  • Live screen-reader demo: open your app in VoiceOver/TalkBack and navigate through it — unlabelled elements make the accessibility guideline instantly real
  • Deepen the fitness tracker GPS discussion from Segment 4 into a full background mode implementation walkthrough using the Expo Location docs
  • Introduce AppState with a live coding example — show the three-line hook that listens for background transitions and logs state changes to the console
  • Explore the expo-task-manager API for background tasks — a natural extension of the background execution discussion

If class is running behind schedule

  • Cut Segment 6 (React Native runtime threading) — important but the least immediately actionable; direct students to read it in the student doc
  • Abbreviate Segment 5 (IPC) to the opening question + one example each for deep links and Share Sheet / Intents, skip the CFU
  • In Segment 7, cover only battery, notifications, and privacy — these have the strongest engagement and most direct project relevance; assign the other five guidelines as reading
  • Move the contract synthesis diagram to a 60-second verbal summary rather than a full discussion

Students who are ahead / want to go deeper

  • Challenge: audit one of their existing projects against all eight responsible development guidelines and produce a written "ecosystem citizenship report" with specific findings
  • Explore: expo-background-fetch and expo-task-manager for background sync patterns
  • Read: Apple's Human Interface Guidelines section on permissions and privacy — primary source for App Store review standards
  • Research: GDPR Article 5 (data minimisation principle) and how it translates to specific mobile app data collection decisions

Students who are struggling with abstract concepts

  • Anchor every concept to the phone in their pocket — ask them to find a concrete example from their own device for each segment
  • Skip the platform comparison panels if iOS/Android differences are causing confusion; the shared concepts are what matter for project work
  • Suggest they draw the sandbox diagram themselves from memory after class — active recall on a spatial diagram is highly effective for this kind of architectural content
  • Pair them with a strong student for the exit question — hearing a peer articulate the concept is often more clarifying than hearing the instructor say it again
