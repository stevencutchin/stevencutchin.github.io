iOS and Android: One document or two? One. The ecosystem concepts on this page — sandboxing, process isolation, shared resources, inter-app communication, and the responsibilities that follow — are fundamentally the same on both platforms. Where iOS and Android differ meaningfully (background execution policy, IPC mechanisms, sandbox strictness), those differences are shown side-by-side using the iOS and Android comparison panels throughout. Comparing them in context teaches more than reading two separate documents ever could.

Your app doesn't run alone. It lives on a device shared with dozens of other apps, all of which want CPU time, memory, network bandwidth, and battery. The OS orchestrates this competition with a strict set of rules — and your app's reputation with users depends entirely on how well you play by them. This lesson maps the ecosystem your app inhabits and establishes the principles of responsible development within it.

1. The Phone as a Shared Environment

A modern smartphone runs dozens of apps simultaneously — or at least appears to. Understanding what "running simultaneously" actually means at the OS level is the foundation for everything else in this lesson. The phone isn't a dedicated computer for your app. It's a shared resource negotiated by the operating system on behalf of many competing tenants.

Figure 1: The phone as a shared city. Each app is a tenant in its own building (sandbox). The OS is the city government — it owns the roads, utilities, and zoning laws that determine what each tenant can do.

At any moment your app may be the active foreground tenant, a suspended background resident, or completely evicted from memory to free up resources for a neighbor. The OS makes these decisions without asking you, and your app must be designed to handle any of these state transitions gracefully.

2. The App Sandbox — Your World Has Walls

Every app on a modern mobile OS lives inside a sandbox: a strictly bounded container that prevents it from accessing the data, files, or memory of any other app. This is a security model, not a limitation to work around. Your users' photos, messages, and banking credentials are protected by the same walls that contain your app.

Figure 2: The app sandbox. Inside the dotted box is your world — private and protected. Shared hardware resources are available only through OS-controlled permission gates. Other app sandboxes are inaccessible directly.

The sandbox is a feature, not a bug. Your users trust that your banking app can't read their health data, and that a sketchy flashlight app can't access their messages. The sandbox is what makes that guarantee possible. When you feel constrained by it, remember: the same walls protect your app's data from every other app on the device.

3. The App Lifecycle — States Your App Passes Through

Unlike a desktop application that runs until the user closes it, a mobile app moves through a carefully managed lifecycle controlled by the OS. Your app can be pushed into any of these states without warning, and you are responsible for handling each transition correctly.

Figure 3: The app lifecycle state machine. The OS moves your app between these states unilaterally. Your job is to save state before transitions and restore it on resume.

In React Native / Expo, you observe lifecycle changes via the AppState API:

📱 Both Platforms — React Native AppState
import { AppState } from 'react-native';

AppState.addEventListener('change', (nextState) => {
  // 'active' | 'background' | 'inactive' (iOS only)
});
Save state before going to background. When your app transitions to background, the OS may terminate it at any time — no further JavaScript will run. If you have unsaved user work (a half-composed form, an in-progress photo edit), listen for the 'background' state and persist it immediately. Don't wait for the user to tap Save.

4. Shared Resources — The Competition for Hardware

Every app on the device draws from the same finite pool of hardware resources. When you drain the battery, hog the CPU, or fill up storage, you're not just hurting your users — you're degrading the experience of every other app they use.

Figure 4: Shared resource pools and platform background execution rules. The battery bar highlights a common mistake — background GPS polling and unthrottled network calls consume a disproportionate share.

5. Inter-App Communication — Talking Across Walls

Apps can't read each other's memory, but they can communicate through OS-mediated channels. These channels are deliberate and controlled — both the sender and receiver must participate explicitly.

Figure 5: Apps communicate only through OS-controlled channels. No app can reach directly into another app's memory — all communication is brokered.
🍎 iOS
Uses URL schemes and Universal Links for deep linking. The Share Sheet is the primary mechanism for passing data between apps. Extensions (Share Extension, Widget, Keyboard) are sandboxed sub-processes of your app.
🤖 Android
Uses Intents — a messaging system that can start activities, services, or broadcast events. More flexible than iOS, but also more complex. Content Providers expose structured data to other apps with fine-grained permission control.

6. The React Native Runtime Environment

Your Expo/React Native app doesn't just run as one thread. It has a specific internal architecture that shapes how you write code and why certain patterns are required.

Figure 6: React Native's multi-threaded runtime. Your JavaScript runs on one thread; native UI on another. Heavy synchronous work on the JS thread blocks rendering. Expensive operations (file I/O, network) are dispatched to background threads.
Why this matters for ecosystem citizenship: A frozen JS thread doesn't just freeze your UI — it can make the entire app feel unresponsive to the OS watchdog, which may report it as "not responding" and offer to kill it. Keep your JS thread free of blocking synchronous work. Use async/await, offload computation with InteractionManager, and never run heavy loops in the event handler of a user gesture.

7. Guidelines for Responsible App Development

Building an app that technically works is the minimum bar. Building an app that is a good citizen of the shared ecosystem — one that respects users' resources, privacy, and trust — is the real standard. These guidelines are not optional niceties; they are the difference between an app that users keep and one they delete.

Figure 7: The eight pillars of responsible app citizenship. A structurally sound app rests on all of them.
🔋

Battery: Be a Tenant, Not a Parasite

GPS, camera, Bluetooth, and constant network polling are the fastest ways to drain a battery. Use the highest-accuracy location mode only when you need it, then downgrade or stop. Batch network requests rather than making a separate HTTP call for every user action. Users notice apps that drain their battery — and they delete them.

🧠

Memory: Return What You Borrow

Images, video buffers, and event listeners that aren't released accumulate and will eventually cause your app to be killed by the OS. Cancel subscriptions in useEffect cleanup functions. Remove event listeners when components unmount. Use FlatList over ScrollView for long lists — it recycles cells. The OS has no swap on mobile; a memory hog gets evicted.

🔐

Privacy: Collect Only What You Need

The principle of minimum necessary data isn't just ethical — it's now legally required in most jurisdictions (GDPR, CCPA, APPI). If your app doesn't need precise GPS coordinates, request "approximate location" instead. If you don't need to store photos on your server, don't. Every piece of user data you collect is a liability as much as it is an asset.

📡

Network: Respect the Radio

Opening a network connection wakes the cellular radio, which takes several seconds to spin up and burns battery even after your request completes. Batch API calls, cache aggressively, and use conditional requests (If-Modified-Since, ETags) to avoid downloading data the device already has. On slow networks, show meaningful loading states — never freeze silently.

🔔

Notifications: Earn Every Interruption

Push notifications are one of the most valuable trust signals users grant to an app. Abusing them — sending marketing noise, over-notifying, waking users at 3am — destroys that trust permanently. Notification permission, once denied, cannot be re-requested programmatically. Treat every notification slot as precious. Ask for permission with a clear explanation of value.

💾

Storage: Clean Up After Yourself

The device's storage is shared across all apps and system data. Temporary files written to the cache directory should be cleaned up when no longer needed — don't assume the OS will do it promptly. Compress images before storing them. If your app allows downloads, give users a way to clear cached content. An app that fills up the user's phone gets deleted.

🔑

Permissions: Ask at the Moment of Need

Requesting camera access the moment the app opens — before the user has any context — is one of the most common causes of permission denial. Users don't grant permissions to apps they don't yet trust. Wait until the user takes an action that requires the permission, then ask with a clear explanation of why it's needed. Never request permissions "just in case" you might need them later.

♿

Accessibility: Design for Everyone

Mobile OS accessibility features — VoiceOver on iOS, TalkBack on Android — are used by millions of people, including users with visual impairments, motor limitations, and temporary situational disabilities (bright sunlight, one hand occupied). Use React Native's accessible, accessibilityLabel, and accessibilityRole props. Test with screen readers on real devices. Accessibility is not a feature — it's a baseline.

The test of a good citizen: Ask yourself — if every app on the device behaved the way mine does, would the device be more or less pleasant to use? Would the battery last longer or shorter? Would the user's attention be more or less respected? If your honest answers are negative, those are engineering decisions worth revisiting.

8. Putting It Together — Your App's Contract with the Ecosystem

Every app implicitly signs a contract with the ecosystem it inhabits. The OS enforces the technical terms — sandbox walls, lifecycle transitions, permission gates. But the ethical and experiential terms are yours to uphold.

Figure 8: The implicit contract every app signs with its ecosystem. The OS enforces the technical terms; the developer is responsible for the ethical ones.
Why this matters beyond ethics: App stores enforce many of these guidelines programmatically. Apps that consume excessive battery are flagged in iOS Screen Time and Android's Digital Wellbeing reports. Apps that request unnecessary permissions are increasingly rejected by App Store and Google Play review teams. In 2024, Apple began automatically revoking permissions for apps that haven't been used in 3 months. The market punishes bad ecosystem citizens just as surely as the OS does.
